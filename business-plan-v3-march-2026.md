# Part 1: Problem Memo

## The pain in one paragraph

A premium hotel generating EUR 15–70M in annual revenue is a medium-sized business operating in a micro-market. Guests arrive from five to eight countries, each traveling for different reasons, each expecting communication in their own language and cultural frame. The marketing department serving this complexity is typically one person with three to five years of experience who speaks two languages. That person handles everything from the Instagram feed to the welcome message on the in-room TV. The chain's headquarters produces brand guidelines and buys media centrally, but cannot produce property-specific content for each hotel's unique guest mix and local market. Agencies deliver generic content that arrives late, reads the same for every property, and costs EUR 3,000–8,000 per month. The result: every hotel communicates generically, misses demand windows, and leaves direct bookings on the table.

## Why existing alternatives fail

Review tools like TrustYou give a 4.3 score and "staff: positive." They do not tell the team what German business travelers actually expect versus Park Hyatt. Reputation platforms track sentiment; they do not produce commercial action. Revenue management systems optimize rates but cannot create the content that gets a traveler to the direct channel in the first place. Agencies produce brand-safe creative that could describe any hotel in the same star category. Generic AI writing tools produce fluent text with no property-specific intelligence behind it.

## What changed: AI discovery is a new threat

The distribution environment shifted in 2025–2026. The strongest evidence: BCG and NYU reported in March 2026 that **37% of travelers** already use AI language models embedded in travel sites to plan and book trips. Cloudbeds found that in AI hotel recommendations, **55.3% of cited sources came from OTAs** and **72.4% of recommended hotels were branded or large-group properties**—meaning independent and premium hotels are systematically disadvantaged. Adobe Analytics measured a **3,500% year-over-year surge** in AI-referred traffic to travel sites.

Directionally useful but less rigorous: Klook/CNBC reported 91% of travelers use AI tools in trip planning (likely inflated by broad definition of "AI tools" including any embedded AI feature). HotelRank.ai claims only 1 in 6 hotels appear in AI-generated recommendations (methodology not fully public). These numbers are directionally consistent with the BCG/NYU and Cloudbeds findings but should be treated as supporting evidence, not primary claims.

This means the hotel's website is no longer the only first impression. The hotel's story is increasingly assembled inside ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and AI-enabled travel surfaces. If the property's information is generic, stale, weakly structured, or missing intent-specific proof, the AI layer fills the gaps with OTA summaries and stronger competitor narratives. The hotel loses control of its own story at the moment travelers are making decisions.

## The four gaps

- **Strategy gap:** The property does not know which guest segments and demand moments matter most right now.
- **Decision gap:** Nobody turns scattered signals into clear priorities fast enough to act within the booking window.
- **Execution gap:** Even when the team knows roughly what to do, getting targeted multilingual content shipped is slow, expensive, and dependent on external vendors.
- **Distribution gap:** Weak or generic content now hurts discoverability in AI-mediated travel planning—not just on Google but across ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, and every AI-enabled booking surface.

## Why this matters financially

A EUR 25M hotel paying 18% OTA commission on 50% of bookings sends **EUR 2.25M per year** to Booking.com and Expedia. Shifting just 5% of OTA bookings to direct saves EUR 112K annually. Meanwhile, the hotel pays EUR 36–96K per year for agency content that is generic and monolingual. The gap between what the hotel spends on commercial execution and what it gets back is where the platform earns its place.

> The problem is not "lack of marketing tools." The problem is that no property-level commercial system exists that can ingest signals, decide what matters, and ship something useful—fast enough to affect bookings, in all the languages and formats the modern traveler expects.

# Part 2: Business Plan

## Section 1: Company Thesis

> The platform helps premium hotels win more direct bookings and higher-value guests by deciding what each property should say, promote, and publish each month—from live market, review, competitor, and AI-discovery signals.

Tercier is a Swiss AG founded to build a vertical AI platform for premium and luxury hotel commercial teams. It gives each property the operating capacity of a stronger local commercial and content team—without adding headcount. The platform reads the market around a property, the guests already coming, the competitors winning nearby, and the way the property appears across direct channels and AI answers. It then decides what matters now and drafts the pages, campaigns, and content the team needs to ship.

## Section 2: Problem Definition

### The current workflow

Sofia is a marketing coordinator at a five-star hotel in Milan. Four years of experience. Reports to the Regional Marketing Director. On a typical Tuesday, she opens Canva to resize a social post the agency sent three weeks late. She answers an email from the regional team asking for a competitor overview that nobody has built because no system produces it. She Googles "events Milan April 2026" to figure out what to push in Friday's newsletter. She writes the newsletter in English, sends it to a translator for Italian and German, gets it back Thursday, loads it into Mailchimp, and blasts it to the full list because nobody has segmented the database by guest type. Open rate: 18%. Booking revenue from email this quarter: near zero.

She knows the hotel has Japanese honeymooners, American luxury families, German business travelers, and Italian weekend couples. She speaks English and Italian. She has never written a word targeting the Japanese or German segments. She has never audited the hotel website through the eyes of a German business traveler because she does not know what one expects from a luxury hotel site. She has no competitive intelligence beyond Instagram and occasional industry events.

### Why it breaks

Each hotel is its own micro-market. Source markets, traveler intents, event calendars, booking windows, languages, competitor sets, and guest expectations differ property by property. Most hotel groups cannot give each property a serious local commercial function. Local teams are thin. HQ support is generic. Agencies are slow. Review tools, reputation tools, revenue tools, and content workflows sit in separate places. The result: missed demand windows, generic property messaging, slow reaction to local events, weak direct-channel execution, and poor visibility in AI-mediated travel discovery.

### Who feels the pain

The property-level marketing person who lacks tools and time. The General Manager who sees OTA commissions eroding margin. The Commercial Director who cannot prove direct-channel ROI. The Regional Marketing Director managing 8–15 properties with agencies producing identical content for each one.

### The economic consequence

Hotels spend 4–6% of revenue on marketing (luxury up to 8%), yet most of this goes to generic output. OTA commissions of 15–30% drain direct-booking potential. h2c found that **43% of hotel chains have no team reviewing automatable work**. 80% of hotels spend up to two full workdays per week pulling reports together. Manual review analysis of 1,700 reviews across four platforms in six languages would take a human analyst 40+ hours—and no single person speaks all six languages.

## Section 4: Why Now

### Five converging shifts

1. **AI discovery is reshaping hotel distribution.** 37% of travelers use AI for trip planning (BCG/NYU March 2026). Early data suggests AI-referred visitors convert at higher rates than organic search—Microsoft Clarity reported 3x, though sample sizes and methodologies vary. Hotels with structured, intent-specific content win in AI answers; those with generic content get replaced by OTA summaries (Cloudbeds: 55% of AI citations go to OTAs).

2. **Hotel adoption readiness is at an inflection.** 78% of chains already use AI, 89% plan expansion in 12–24 months (h2c 2025). But true reliance is still low (4.7/10) and only 7% have a company-wide AI strategy. The market is ready to buy but does not know what to buy.

3. **Model costs collapsed.** Inference costs dropped 30–50% per year for equivalent capability. What was economically unviable two years ago—processing 2,000+ reviews in six languages, generating property-specific content at scale—now costs under EUR 10 per hotel per month in AI inference.

4. **Agency model is breaking.** Hotels pay EUR 3,000–8,000/month per property for content that arrives late, reads the same for every property, and is monolingual. A platform that produces better, faster, multilingual output for EUR 1,000–5,000/month is a cost reduction with a quality upgrade.

5. **Direct-booking urgency is intensifying.** OTA commissions remain 15–30%. Every percentage point of OTA-to-direct shift is pure margin. Hotels are actively seeking anything that strengthens the direct channel—but they lack the commercial machinery to execute.

## Section 5: Product Thesis

### What the platform is

The platform is a property-level commercial execution system for premium and luxury hotels. It is not a dashboard and it is not a report. It is an orchestrated system of seven layers that reads the market, understands the guests, evaluates the competition, monitors AI discovery, decides what matters now, and drafts the assets the team needs to ship.

### The seven layers

**1. Market and location intelligence.** The platform identifies the property's commercial context: source markets, local demand drivers, seasonality, booking windows, major events and compression periods, and the property-specific competitive set. This gives each hotel the local commercial reality that generic tools miss.

**2. Voice-of-customer intelligence.** The platform ingests review and guest-feedback signals across languages and platforms, extracting recurring strengths and weaknesses, traveler motivations, unmet expectations, phrases guests actually use, and proof points that matter by segment. This turns reviews into commercial input, not just sentiment scores.

**3. Persona and intent modeling.** The platform builds property-specific demand models around real guest types. These are not broad templates like "business traveler." The platform models who actually chooses this property, why they choose it, what anxieties they have before booking, which claims they trust, and which competitor messages outperform the property for that intent.

**4. Competitive reading.** The platform reads the property and its comp set through the lens of a chosen guest type or traveler intent. The team sees where the property is under-explaining value, where a competitor is clearer, which proof points are missing, and which pages or messages are weak for a specific segment.

**5. AI discovery and visibility intelligence.** The platform monitors how the property is represented in AI-mediated discovery—ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, AI-enabled travel surfaces—and where the narrative breaks. It identifies OTA dominance over the hotel's own narrative, missing answers to common traveler questions, stale or generic descriptions, weak machine-readable property facts, and missing segment-specific content. This layer protects the direct channel when travelers ask AI where to stay.

**6. Decision and prioritization engine.** The platform combines market, review, persona, comp-set, and AI-discovery signals into a ranked execution queue. It decides which guest segments matter now, which demand moments deserve action, which messages are underperforming, which new pages should exist, and which updates are most likely to improve direct demand and visibility.

**7. Content and asset engine.** The platform drafts the assets the team needs to ship: landing pages, event and seasonal pages, suite and room copy, newsletters and campaign inputs, OTA and directory copy, FAQ blocks and answer assets, multilingual variations by segment and channel. These assets are shaped for both human conversion and AI retrieval—using clear entity naming, self-contained answer blocks, review-grounded proof points, extractable formats, structured data hooks, and freshness markers.

### What the human does vs. what the AI does

The AI reads, analyzes, decides, and drafts. The human reviews, adjusts, approves, and publishes. The marketing person stops being a researcher, writer, and translator. They become an editor and decision-maker.

### The dual value proposition

The platform solves two problems with one system. The **content problem**: hotels cannot produce property-specific, persona-targeted, multilingual content at the speed and volume required. The **discovery problem**: hotels are losing narrative control in AI-mediated travel planning. The same structured, intent-specific, proof-backed content that converts a human guest is also the content that AI answer engines can understand, trust, and reuse.

## Section 7: Competitive Landscape

The platform does not compete primarily against other AI startups. It competes against the absence of a property-level commercial team. The real "competitor" is the 28-year-old marketing coordinator who used to work at a travel agency and now writes the welcome message on the TV screen. That person is the entire marketing department of a EUR 25M hotel.

| Category | Examples | What they do | What they miss |
|---|---|---|---|
| Review/reputation tools | TrustYou, ReviewPro, Revinate | Track sentiment scores and respond to reviews | No commercial action, no content, no competitive reading through guest lens |
| Agency content | Local/regional agencies | Produce brand-safe creative, social content, newsletters | Generic, slow, monolingual, same for every property, EUR 3–8K/mo |
| AI content tools | Jasper, Copy.ai, ChatGPT direct | Generate fluent marketing text | No property intelligence, no guest data, no competitive context, no prioritization |
| Hotel marketing SaaS | Cendyn, Sojern, Lighthouse | CRM, programmatic ads, rate intelligence | Siloed functions, no content execution, no persona-driven strategy |
| AI visibility / booking channel | HotelRank.ai, HotelWorld AI, The Hotels Network | Monitor or enable AI search presence and ChatGPT booking | Monitoring or distribution only—no property-level content generation, no competitive reading, no commercial strategy layer |
| Full-stack hotel platforms | Cloudbeds, Canary Technologies ($80M Series D, 20K+ hotels) | Broad PMS-centric platforms with AI guest management | PMS-first, operations-focused. Not solving the marketing/content gap. Different buyer (IT/ops) and budget. |
| HQ/chain support | Brand marketing teams | Brand guidelines, media buying, chain-wide campaigns | Cannot serve each property's unique guest mix and local market |

### Competitive motion worth watching

Several players are moving toward the AI discovery channel. Lighthouse is shipping ChatGPT-native hotel booking flows. The Hotels Network launched a ChatGPT direct-booking app. HotelWorld AI is specifically targeting AI search visibility. These moves validate the thesis that AI discovery matters—but they are distribution plays, not content and intelligence plays. They help a hotel show up in an AI answer; they do not help the hotel figure out what to say when it gets there.

Canary Technologies is the most funded player ($80M Series D, $600M valuation, June 2025), but they are PMS-centric with AI guest management—different buyer, different budget, and no content execution layer.

The deeper risk is not any single competitor. It is that the absence of a dominant player might mean the full-stack need is less budget-coherent than assumed—hotels may prefer to buy point tools and agencies separately rather than one platform. This is why the proof-of-value tier exists: to validate that buyers want the orchestration, not just one layer.

No existing tool combines property-level market intelligence, voice-of-customer analysis, persona-driven competitive reading, AI discovery monitoring, commercial prioritization, and multilingual content execution in one platform. The value lies in the orchestration—not in any single layer. But the orchestration must prove its worth against the mix-and-match alternative.

## Section 8: Go-to-Market Model

### Phase 1: Founder-led sales + partner network (months 1–12)

The first hotels are won through direct relationships. Amedeo Guffanti and Marco Corsaro bring decades of senior hospitality and consulting experience and direct access to European luxury hotel decision-makers. **[INSERT GROUP NAME]** has expressed interest in a 2026 pilot (introduced through Guffanti's network). Initial sales focus on Switzerland and northern Italy, leveraging the Swiss dataset of 649 four- and five-star hotels with identified contacts. Target: 2–5 paying hotels by month 6, 15–21 by month 12 (see Execution Roadmap, Sales Funnel, and Hiring Plan for aligned milestones).

### Phase 2: Partner-led expansion (months 6–18)

Guffanti's and Corsaro's relationships open doors across European hotel groups. The expansion motion follows a land-and-expand pattern: prove value at one property, expand to sister properties. Regional and cluster marketing directors who manage 5–15 properties are the expansion buyers—when one Sofia can produce targeted multilingual content for five guest segments in a morning, the director wants it for every property.

### Phase 3: Direct + channel (months 18–36, 70–150+ hotels)

Once 25+ properties are active and the first sales hire is in place (month 18–20), the platform has case studies and ROI proof. Sales motion expands to direct outreach, industry events (ITB Berlin, IHIF, HITEC), and strategic partnerships with hospitality consultancies and PMS providers.

### What hotel number one gets in the first 30 days

Week 1: The platform ingests publicly available reviews (Booking, Google, TripAdvisor, Expedia), maps the competitive set, and builds initial personas from existing signals. No PMS integration required. The team receives a competitive reading: how the property explains its value versus the comp set, through the eyes of each guest segment.

Week 2–3: First content deliverables ship. A persona-targeted landing page draft for the highest-opportunity segment. An AI discovery audit showing how the property appears in ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity—and where the narrative breaks. A multilingual content brief for the next campaign cycle.

Week 4: The monthly commercial brief arrives: what changed in the market, which segments are moving, what the comp set published, which content priorities the platform recommends for the next 30 days. The marketing person has gone from "researching what to do" to "reviewing and approving what's already drafted."

The proof metric before direct-booking lag shows up: number of persona-targeted assets shipped, time-to-publish versus baseline, and content adoption rate (what percentage of platform-generated content the team actually uses).

### The demo that closes the sale

Amedeo sits with the Commercial Director at a Zurich five-star hotel. He does not show her a dashboard. He does not explain seven layers. He shows her one thing.

The platform evaluates her hotel's website through the eyes of a German business traveler — the hotel's largest source market — and displays the result side by side with the Park Hyatt Zurich. She sees where her property under-explains what German travelers care about (parking, spa, quiet rooms, efficient check-in), where the Park Hyatt is clearer, which proof points from her own guest reviews she is not using, and which pages are missing entirely. Then the platform switches to a Chinese honeymooner's perspective. Her site has no Chinese-language content. The competitor does. That is the moment she leans forward.

This is the one demo Amedeo needs to promise in every introduction meeting: "We will show you your own hotel through the eyes of your most valuable guest, next to the hotel that is beating you for that guest. Fifteen minutes. If you do not see something you did not know, we stop there."

The objection that will come: "This sounds like ChatGPT with extra steps." The answer: ChatGPT knows nothing about her hotel. It does not know that her German guests care about parking and spa quality. It does not know that her Chinese guests love the lake view rooms. It does not know that the Bürgenstock has a Chinese-language website and she does not. The platform knows all of this because it read her reviews and her competitors' reviews. ChatGPT writes generic copy. This writes copy about her hotel, for her guests, based on what her guests actually said.

### Deployment motion

**Technical setup** takes 3–5 hours per hotel: one onboarding call, one platform walkthrough, one check-in. The AI agents self-provision—reviews are ingested automatically, personas built by compute, competitive set mapped without manual data entry. No PMS integration required.

**Managed onboarding** for the first 5–10 luxury customers adds 1–2 weeks of active founder involvement on top of the technical setup: brand voice calibration, competitive set validation with the hotel team, content quality review and approval cycles. This is functionally services work in the pilot phase. The goal is to encode these patterns into the platform so customer 15 onboards in 3–5 hours like software, not like a consulting engagement. By customer 20, the managed layer should be systematized into a self-serve flow.

## Section 9: Business Model and Pricing

### Pricing model

Per-property monthly subscription. The pricing metric is per hotel because the value compounds at the property level and the buyer thinks in property-level budgets.

| Tier | Per property/month | What the hotel gets |
|---|---|---|
| Proof-of-value (90 days, then converts) | EUR 1,000–1,500 | Time-limited entry: market signals, review analysis, persona models, competitive reading, AI discovery audit. Converts to Intelligence tier after 90 days or terminates. Not a permanent price point. |
| Intelligence | EUR 1,500–2,500 | Full platform: market data, review analysis, comp-set mapping, persona builder, evaluation engine, AI visibility monitoring, monthly commercial brief |
| Intelligence + Content | EUR 2,500–5,000 | All above plus content engine, multilingual generation, content calendar, campaign inputs, answer assets |
| Group rollout (5+ properties) | EUR 2,000–4,000 per property | Full platform with cross-property benchmarking and portfolio reporting |

### Why this pricing works

**Replacement math:** Hotels currently pay EUR 3,000–8,000/month for generic agency content. The platform produces better, faster, persona-targeted, multilingual output for EUR 2,500–5,000. It is a cost reduction with a quality upgrade.

**Capability math:** Hiring a senior marketer, market analyst, and multilingual content lead costs EUR 180–300K/year. The platform provides equivalent capability for EUR 30–60K/year.

**Commission math:** A EUR 25M hotel shifting 5% of OTA bookings to direct saves EUR 112K/year. The platform costs EUR 30–60K/year. Commission savings alone return 2–4x the investment.

**Revenue math:** A properly segmented newsletter generates USD 1,249 average booking value per conversion (Revinate 2025). Targeted segments under 5,000 contacts convert at 8x the rate of blast emails. The hotel that goes from one generic monthly blast to four persona-targeted multilingual campaigns per month is operating in a different category.

Task routing determines cost: extraction and classification tasks go to fast, cheap models (Haiku 4.5, Gemini Flash 3.1); strategic analysis, persona modeling, and content generation use mid-tier frontier models (Sonnet 4.5, GPT-5.4 mini); quality-critical luxury content and complex competitive evaluations route to top-tier models (Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, Gemini 3.1 Pro) only when needed. This isn't theoretical—the Loamly platform already runs this routing pattern in production.

### Why the margin structure holds

The structural advantage is real: per-customer AI costs are 1–2.5% of revenue because the platform processes publicly available data (no expensive data licensing), routes tasks to cost-appropriate models (cheap models for extraction, frontier for strategy and content), and per-hotel compute does not scale linearly with price—a EUR 5,000/month account uses similar compute to a EUR 1,500/month account. Model costs decline 30–50% per year at equivalent capability, improving margins even as the platform ships more capability.

The honest risk: at 5 hotels on proof-of-value pricing, the company operates at a loss (–36%). This is a short window—cash-flow breakeven arrives at ~7 hotels at proof-of-value pricing, or ~5 hotels at full pricing. The AI-native operating model (no contractors, no premature hires) means fixed costs are only ~EUR 8,200/month. From breakeven, margins expand rapidly: ~33–47% at 25 hotels (with first two hires absorbing margin), ~66–75% at 100 hotels with a full 8-person team as revenue outpaces headcount costs. The path depends on growing revenue against fixed costs—not on squeezing per-customer COGS.

### Churn and net revenue retention

**Annual logo churn assumption: 5–8%.** Premium hotels with EUR 2,000+/month ACV, embedded brand voice calibration, accumulated competitive benchmarks, and historical persona data have high switching costs—they would need to rebuild all of this from scratch with a new provider. This is not SMB self-serve SaaS where users cancel after a free trial. The comparable vertical SaaS benchmarks (TrustYou, Revinate, Cendyn) operate in the 5–10% annual churn range for their premium tiers.

At 100 hotels, 5–8% churn means 5–8 replacements per year—easily covered by the sales pipeline at that stage (the funnel adds ~2 hotels/month even in the solo-founder phase). The real risk is not logo churn but contraction: hotels downgrading from Intelligence + Content to Intelligence-only. Mitigation: the content engine is the stickiest layer (it produces the assets the team actually ships every month), and the proof-of-value phase validates willingness-to-pay before the hotel commits to a full subscription.

**Net revenue retention target: 110–120%.** Expansion revenue comes from three sources: tier upgrades (Intelligence → Intelligence + Content, +EUR 1,000–2,500/month), group rollouts (one property → sister properties within the same chain, 5–15x deal expansion), and annual price increases (3–5% at renewal). At 110% NRR, the company grows revenue from existing customers by 10% annually before any new sales—offsetting churn entirely and contributing to compounding growth.

**Impact on Stage C projections:** At 100 hotels with 7% logo churn and 115% NRR, the net effect is positive: the company loses ~7 hotels but gains ~15% more revenue from existing customers through expansion. The 100-hotel base at EUR 250K/month MRR would grow to ~EUR 287K/month from expansion alone, while replacing the 7 churned hotels requires ~4 months of pipeline at steady-state acquisition rates.

### Implementation burden

**At scale:** Low. The platform ingests publicly available signals. No PMS integration required for the first wedge. No forward-deployed engineers per customer. Technical onboarding: 3–5 hours per property (one call, one walkthrough, one check-in).

**Early stage:** Moderate. Technical setup is 3–5 hours, but the first 5–10 luxury customers require an additional 1–2 weeks of managed engagement on top: brand voice calibration, competitive set validation, content quality approval, and initial persona review with the hotel team. This is functionally services work. The a16z warning applies: AI application companies often need services and forward deployment early. The goal is to encode these patterns into the platform so customer 15+ onboards like software, not like a consulting engagement.

## Section 11: Moat and Compounding Advantage

### What gets better with each customer

- **Prompt and evaluation quality:** Every property interaction improves how the platform models personas, evaluates competitive positioning, and generates content. Prompts, rubrics, and quality benchmarks compound across the portfolio.
- **Competitive intelligence density:** As more properties in a market join the platform, the competitive reading becomes richer. The platform builds the most complete view of how premium hotels position themselves across markets, segments, and languages.
- **Content pattern library:** Successful content patterns—which headlines convert for German business travelers, which proof points resonate with Japanese honeymooners—accumulate into a proprietary asset that no single hotel or agency possesses.
- **AI discovery benchmarks:** Cross-property data on what makes hotels visible in AI answers creates a unique dataset that improves recommendations for every customer.

### Switching costs

Once a hotel's commercial workflow runs through the platform—personas built, competitive benchmarks established, content calendar operational, AI visibility tracked—switching means rebuilding all of this from scratch. The platform becomes the commercial operating system, not a point tool.

### What competitors cannot easily copy

The orchestration. Any single layer (review analysis, content generation, competitive monitoring) can be built in isolation. The value is in how they connect: reviews feed personas, personas guide competitive reading, competitive reading informs priorities, priorities drive content, content is shaped for both human conversion and AI retrieval. Replicating the orchestration requires deep domain expertise and extensive iteration on hospitality-specific workflows.

## Section 12: Execution Roadmap

The roadmap below aligns with the hiring plan (Part 4), financial tables (Section 10.6), and growth strategy. Each stage's hotel count, MRR range, and team size are consistent across all sections. Month ranges are targets — actual hiring and expansion decisions are triggered by MRR milestones and specific operational bottlenecks, not by the calendar (see AI-native hiring triggers in Part 4).

### What exists on day one — and how the product gets to market

The platform does not start from zero. Loamly's architecture — dashboard framework, AI query pipeline, multi-model orchestration, data ingestion patterns, auth, billing, multi-tenant data layer — transfers directly. What is new is the hospitality-specific intelligence layer: review ingestion across platforms and languages, guest persona modeling, competitive set reading, AI discovery monitoring, and multilingual content generation. Building this layer is the core work of months 1–3.

The approach is deliberate: sell before the product is finished, deliver value from day one, and productize based on what hotels actually use.

**Months 1–2: Concierge MVP.** The first 1–3 hotels receive intelligence delivered with high founder involvement. The AI pipelines run, the founder curates and quality-checks the output, and the hotel sees results through a simple dashboard — or in the earliest days, a branded report. What the commercial director receives: guest personas derived from thousands of reviews, competitive positioning analysis, AI visibility assessment, and 5–10 content briefs for the next month. The delivery is high-touch. The value is real — no hotel has this today at any price. The proof-of-value pricing (EUR 1,000–1,500/month) reflects the early-stage product honestly: below the full Intelligence tier, but high enough to signal genuine commercial value and filter out hotels that are not serious buyers.

**Months 3–4: Pattern recognition.** After 3 hotels, the founder knows which workflows drive the most value versus which the hotel ignores. The top 3–5 workflows get productized into the self-serve dashboard. Everything else remains semi-automated with founder oversight. This is the critical learning phase — the product roadmap is shaped by observed behavior, not assumptions.

**Months 5–8: Production platform.** The core loop runs automatically: ingest → analyze → recommend → draft. The human-in-the-loop shifts from the founder to the hotel's marketing person, who reviews and approves rather than waiting for founder-delivered output. This is when the platform can scale from 5 to 15 hotels without requiring 80-hour weeks.

**Why this works as a solo operation in the build phase.** Building the hospitality intelligence layer is an AI engineering task, not a team coordination task. One developer with deep context across the entire codebase makes faster, more coherent architectural decisions than a two-person team splitting the work and spending hours on code review, design alignment, and merge conflicts. The first 3–5 hotels need a founder who can sit with a commercial director, understand what she actually needs, walk back to the laptop, and build it that afternoon. That feedback loop — customer conversation to shipped feature in hours — is the single biggest advantage of the solo-founder phase. It is impossible with a team and impossible to replicate later. Hiring an engineer at month 2 means spending months 2–5 on onboarding, architecture debates, and coordination overhead instead of shipping. The first technical hire should inherit a working product with clear architectural patterns, not help figure out what to build.

4. **AI SaaS economics.** Bootstrapped companies now match VC-backed growth rates (28% YoY median) while retaining 80–95% equity. For a vertical SaaS with EUR 2,000/month ACV and high gross margins, this is the superior path.

1. **Cash reserve: 3–6 months of operating costs (~EUR 250–500K).** Standard for a healthy SaaS company. Ensures the company never needs emergency fundraising and can weather a bad quarter without cutting staff.

2. **Growth reinvestment.** New market entry (Middle East, Asia-Pacific), product R&D (PMS integrations, agent-readiness layers), additional hires beyond the 8-person plan, event sponsorships and booth presence at ITB/IHIF/HITEC. This is where most of the surplus goes in years 2–3.

3. **Series A war chest.** If the shareholders decide to raise institutional capital, having EUR 1–2M in the bank while generating EUR 3M+ ARR with 66% margins is an exceptionally strong negotiating position. Fundraising becomes optional, not survival.

4. **Shareholder dividends.** A Swiss AG can distribute retained earnings as dividends. Swiss withholding tax of 35% applies (refundable for Swiss tax residents via annual tax return). Most growth-stage companies retain 80–100% of profits and defer dividends until growth stabilizes. The shareholders can decide annually whether to distribute a portion or reinvest everything. With only three shareholders and no VC board pressure, this is a genuine option — but growth reinvestment is the default in years 1–3.

5. **Founder compensation adjustment.** The plan already includes a 2% MRR revenue-share component (EUR 5,000/month at EUR 250K MRR). Beyond this, the board can approve salary increases or performance bonuses from retained earnings once the company is comfortably profitable. The guiding principle: founder compensation scales with the business, not ahead of it.

**The bottom line:** In the solo-founder phase, there is no surplus — the seed covers costs until breakeven. In the growth phase, surplus funds hires and expansion. In the scaled phase, the board decides between reinvestment, reserves, dividends, or Series A positioning. Every euro is accounted for — retained earnings are a balance-sheet asset of the AG, visible in the annual financial statements and subject to shareholder approval at the annual general meeting.

**The 100–150 hotel target—is it enough?** 100 hotels in 36 months represents 30% penetration of the Swiss premium segment (329 hotels at ADR ≥ CHF 250) and 15% of all Swiss 4–5 star hotels (649)—noting that expansion into northern Italy and DACH will contribute significantly to the count. By hotel tech standards, this is ambitious: Mews achieved 276 properties by year 4 (but targeting global SMB, not premium-only). For a premium-focused vertical SaaS in its first 3 years, 100–150 is aggressive but achievable given the sales funnel math (21 hotels by month 12 solo, with group deals and a sales hire accelerating from month 18). The real metric investors will care about is ARR and growth rate: EUR 2.4–4.5M ARR with 60–100% YoY growth and 66–75% operating margins is a strong Series A story.

The 100–150 hotel target also undersells the revenue potential. Expansion revenue within existing accounts (upselling from Intelligence to Intelligence + Content tier, group rollouts to sister properties) can add 30–50% to ARR without new customer acquisition. If 25% of hotels upgrade from EUR 1,500 to EUR 3,500/month and 20% expand to 3+ sister properties, the effective ARR at 100–150 hotels is EUR 3.1–6.8M.
