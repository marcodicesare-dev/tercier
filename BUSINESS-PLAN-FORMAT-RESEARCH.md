# 1. BOTTOM LINE — THE FORMAT THAT WORKS {#1-bottom-line}

The best 2026 format for a vertical AI business plan is **not** a classic MBA-style 20-40 page plan and **not** a generic pitch deck turned into prose.

The strongest format is a **4-part system:**

1. **Investment-memo style operating plan** — 14 sections, plain language, forces clarity on AI-specific economics
2. **Standard Business Model Canvas** — the Strategyzer 9-block, not a gimmick replacement
3. **AI-specific overlay** — one page covering automation boundary, data rights, model stack, implementation intensity, gross margin path, reusability
4. **Assumption register** — every claim tagged with evidence, confidence, and validation method

This is the only format that fits how vertical AI companies are actually being built and judged in 2025-2026. The winning format is less about visual polish and more about forcing brutal clarity.

### Why Memo > Slides > PDF

- **Memo** forces plain language, reveals weak assumptions quickly, handles AI business-model nuance better than slides. Works for founder clarity, partner alignment, AND investor conversations.
- **Slides** (pitch deck) are a companion artifact — the Narrative Deck for cold send, not the primary thinking tool.
- **PDF business plans** in the classic sense are dead for AI companies. No one reads 40 pages.

### The One Test

If someone reads Section 2 (Problem) and it sounds like a consultant wrote it, the whole plan is wrong. It should sound like the buyer.

# 2. WHY CLASSIC FORMATS FAIL FOR AI VERTICALS {#2-why-classic-fails}

Classic startup business plans over-index on:
- TAM theater (massive number, no bottom-up validation)
- Vague product descriptions ("AI-powered platform for...")
- Pretty pricing tiers (3-column SaaS table with no unit economics)
- Generic go-to-market ("partner-led + direct sales")

They underweight the things that now decide whether the business is real:
- How much workflow you truly replace
- Whether the company is a platform or a services-heavy wedge
- What deployment burden exists per customer
- What gross margin path looks like (and what drives COGS)
- How the product becomes a system of work instead of an AI wrapper

**Source pressure:**
- a16z: AI application companies often need services and forward deployment early. Optimizing for early gross margin alone is the wrong lens.
- a16z: Many companies pitch a Palantir-style model without clear product boundaries or repeatability.
- ICONIQ + Bessemer: Hybrid pricing, margin pressure, and application-layer differentiation are now core facts.
- Strategyzer: Desirability, feasibility, and viability hypotheses must be explicit and tested.

# 3. THE OPTIMAL BUSINESS PLAN STRUCTURE (14 SECTIONS) {#3-plan-structure}

Use an **investment-memo style operating plan.** Not a brochure. Not a pitch deck in prose.

## Section 1: One-Sentence Company Thesis

Answer in a single sentence:
- Who the buyer is
- What painful workflow is broken
- What measurable outcome improves

If this sentence is weak, the whole plan is weak.

**YC equivalent:** "What is your company going to make?" Paul Graham says the best answers are matter-of-fact. Zero marketing-speak. "A database with a wiki-like interface" beats "We are going to transform the relationship between individuals and information."

## Section 2: Problem Definition

Include:
- The current workflow (what actually happens today, step by step)
- Why it breaks (not "it's inefficient" — specifics)
- Who feels the pain (job title, daily reality)
- What the economic consequence is ($ lost, hours wasted, revenue missed)

**This section should sound like the buyer, not like a consultant.**

## Section 3: ICP and Beachhead Wedge

Define:
- First buyer (who signs the check)
- First user (who uses the product daily)
- First vertical subsegment (which hotels, which chains, which geography)
- Why this segment is ideal now

**This section should explicitly reject adjacent segments.** "We are NOT doing X, Y, Z because..."

Bessemer's entry point ranking:
1. **Tertiary workflows (safer wedge):** Fast sales cycles, but must expand to core
2. **Adjacent-to-core (balanced):** Stronger reliability proof needed
3. **Core workflows (Toast playbook):** Requires undeniable miracle

## Section 4: Why Now

For AI verticals, cover:
- Workflow shift (what changed in how hotels do marketing)
- Adoption shift (ChatGPT Agent Mode, AI travel planning at 25%)
- Data / integration maturity (APIs, PMS connectivity)
- Regulatory / buyer timing (hotel budgets, 77% allocating to AI)
- Model-cost or capability changes (GPT-5-nano at $0.025/MTok)

**Every successful funded company has a clear "Why Now."** It's the 4th slide investors look at (DocSend data), and it gets 10 seconds of attention — make it sharp.

## Section 5: Product Thesis

Describe:
- What system of work you are building (not features)
- What the human does vs. what the AI does
- What is automated vs. reviewed
- Where integrations matter

**For AI companies this is more important than a feature list.** ICONIQ data: 49% of AI companies differentiate at the application layer (UX, workflows, integrations), only 14% at the model layer.

Bessemer's "Progressive Delegation Model": AI doesn't need to fully replace a workflow. Start by automating the most time-intensive, lowest-value slice. Let humans handle the rest. Gradually expand. This creates expansion revenue.

## Section 6: Market Analysis

Must include:
- Market definition (what market are you actually in?)
- Top-down TAM
- Bottom-up TAM (how many hotels × price × attach rate)
- SAM (addressable given your go-to-market)
- Realistic 3-year SOM
- Growth drivers
- Reasons the market is structurally attractive

**Be honest about assumptions and confidence.** Sequoia says: "Some of the best companies invent their own markets."

Key hospitality numbers for calibration:
- $1.7T travel & hospitality revenue (Houlihan Lokey)
- RMS penetration only 28% — massive greenfield
- 77% of hoteliers planning AI investment
- Hotel tech stocks significantly outperformed S&P 500

## Section 7: Competitive Landscape

Must cover:
- Direct competitors (other AI tools)
- Incumbent software (legacy PMS, CRM)
- Agencies / consultants (Jakala, Publicis, etc.)
- Manual workflows (the junior marketer doing everything)
- Non-consumption (hotels doing nothing)

**For vertical AI, the real competitor is often not another AI startup.** Bessemer: Vertical AI competes for LABOR budgets, not IT budgets. The $50K/year junior marketer is the true competitive benchmark.

## Section 8: Go-to-Market Model

Must answer:
- How the first 10 customers are won (founder-led + partner network)
- Whether this is founder-led sales, partner-led, or product-led
- What deployment motion exists
- What proof is required before expansion

Include time-to-value and sales-cycle logic. a16z data: 70% of enterprise buyers say speed of deployment is the #1 factor. Enterprise buyers only want to test AI ONCE.

## Section 9: Business Model and Pricing

Must answer:
- What the pricing metric is (per hotel, per market, per query)
- What is fixed vs. variable
- Whether services are included
- Whether pricing is seat, usage, outcome, or hybrid
- Why this model matches the customer's economics

**ICONIQ 2026 data:** 92.4% of AI companies abandoning flat-rate pricing. 58% still have subscription component. 35% consumption-based (up from 19%). 18% outcome-based (up from 2%). 37% plan to CHANGE pricing in next 12 months.

For AI companies, a generic 3-tier SaaS table is not credible.

## Section 10: Unit Economics and AI Economics

**This is the most missing section in bad AI plans.**

Must include:
- Gross margin today
- Gross margin target (trajectory matters: ICONIQ shows 41% → 52% industry-wide)
- Main COGS drivers (inference, support, deployment)
- Implementation burden per customer
- Human-in-the-loop burden
- Model routing and margin levers
- What mature-customer economics should look like

**Key benchmarks:**
- AI gross margins: 52% in 2026 (ICONIQ, 300 companies), up from 41% in 2024
- Inference = 23% of revenue on average (ICONIQ)
- Bessemer "Shooting Stars" prefer 60%+ margins
- a16z says optimize for total gross profit, not margin percentage
- Rule of 60 replacing Rule of 40 for AI-native companies (Kellblog)

## Section 11: Moat and Compounding Advantage

Must answer:
- What gets better with each customer
- What is reusable vs. bespoke
- Where switching costs come from
- What competitors can copy vs. cannot

**a16z's Seven Powers applied to AI (Mar 2026):**
1. **Switching costs** — declining, but creative destruction, not industry death
2. **Network effects** — NOT going away, get STRONGER with AI
3. **Scale** — matters MORE for AI (compute costs favor larger token consumers)
4. **Brand** — endures and may INCREASE as market gets crowded
5. **Cornered resources** (proprietary data) — "extremely valuable" because AI does more with quality data
6. **Process power** — "perhaps the strongest moat of all"
7. **Counterpositioning** — creates opportunity for startups

Redpoint's take: "We may have unrealistic expectations around moats. In SaaS, nobody would say after 9-10 months you should have a defined moat." Focus on PATH to moat, not current moat.

## Section 12: Execution Roadmap

Include:
- 12-month milestones
- 24-month milestones
- What must be true before hiring, expansion, or fundraising

**Outcome-based, not feature-based.** "10 hotels generating AI-localized content in 3 languages" not "Launch content module v2."

Bessemer: "The window to establish category leadership is measured in quarters, not years."

## Section 13: Risks and Kill Criteria

Must include:
- What could break the thesis
- What assumptions might be false
- What evidence would cause a pivot

**This section is a sign of seriousness, not weakness.** YC advice: "Better to disclose all flaws than conceal them — if we think of a problem you don't mention, we'll assume it's because you haven't thought of it."

## Section 14: Validation Backlog

Every AI vertical plan should end with:
- Open questions
- Experiments to run
- Evidence needed to de-risk the plan

# 4. THE BUSINESS MODEL CANVAS + AI OVERLAY {#4-bmc}

## Core Rule

Do NOT invent a brand-new AI canvas. Use the **standard Strategyzer Business Model Canvas** — the 9-block structure:
- Customer Segments
- Value Propositions
- Channels
- Customer Relationships
- Revenue Streams
- Key Resources
- Key Activities
- Key Partners
- Cost Structure

## The AI-Specific Overlay (One Page)

The standard BMC alone is insufficient for vertical AI. Add a one-page overlay with six fields:

### 1. Automation Boundary
What the AI does autonomously, what humans review, what remains manual. This is the single most important thing to make explicit.

### 2. Data Rights and Data Sources
What data is proprietary, customer-owned, licensed, public, or inferred. Where does the training/fine-tuning data come from?

### 3. Model Stack and Provider Dependence
Which providers power the system, where routing happens, failure/dependency risks. Multi-model is now standard (3.1 providers average per ICONIQ).

### 4. Implementation Intensity
How much custom setup per customer, and how that changes over time. If every deployment is unique, it's a services business. a16z's test: "What breaks if you sign 50 customers next year?"

### 5. Gross Margin Path
What margin looks like at pilot, 10 customers, 50 customers, mature accounts. Must show trajectory, not just current state.

### 6. Reusability vs. Bespoke Work
What percent of deployment is reusable primitives vs. customer-specific customization. This is what separates a product company from a consulting firm.

## How to Use the Canvas

Do NOT fill it as a static workshop artifact. Use it as:
- A compression tool after research
- A consistency check against the plan
- A way to expose contradictions

**Contradictions it should reveal:**
- Value prop says "platform," cost structure says "consulting"
- Revenue stream says "software multiple," delivery model says "custom projects"
- Moat says "data," but no data rights exist
- Key Activities says "automated," but Cost Structure has 60% labor

# 5. THE 4-PART ARTIFACT SYSTEM {#5-artifact-system}

The strongest stack is a **4-part system**, not one document:

## Part 1: Problem Memo (1-2 pages)
Short narrative answering:
- Who hurts
- Why now
- Why existing alternatives fail

This stands alone. If this memo doesn't make someone say "yes, that's exactly right," the rest doesn't matter.

## Part 2: AI Business Plan Memo (14 sections above)
The main document. 15-25 pages maximum.

## Part 3: Business Model Canvas + AI Overlay (2 pages)
One page for the standard BMC. One page for the AI overlay.

## Part 4: Assumption Register (table)
Columns:
- Assumption
- Category: desirability / feasibility / viability
- Evidence (what we know)
- Confidence: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW / SPECULATIVE
- Validation method (how to test)

**That last artifact is what prevents AI-business-plan bullshit.** Every claim in the plan should trace back to an entry here.

# 7. VC EVALUATION FRAMEWORKS — WHAT THEY ACTUALLY SCORE ON {#7-vc-frameworks}

## Bessemer's "Good, Better, Best" for Vertical AI

| Dimension | Good | Better | Best |
|---|---|---|---|
| Functional Value | New feature that demos well | New feature with hard ROI | End-to-end workflow with "LLM magic" not previously possible |
| Economic Value | Productivity boost | OpEx cost reduction | Revenue boost for HIGH-VALUE labor |
| Competitive Dynamics | SaaS incumbent or weak AI | Adjacent / sleepy incumbent | No legitimate modern incumbent |
| Defensibility | Execution speed | Complex + early data moat | TRUE moat: data + multimodality |

**Core thesis:** "Vertical AI competes for LABOR budgets (13% of US GDP), not IT budgets. That's 10x the software market."

## Bessemer's Two-Tier Benchmark

**"Supernovas"** — hypergrowth, low/negative margins, "may not align with long-term value"
**"Shooting Stars"** — ~60% margins, sustainable growth, better retention

**Bessemer prefers Shooting Stars.**

## Redpoint's Three-Question Framework

1. **Effective AI wedge?** Bar for PMF has gone UP.
2. **How much expansion from this wedge?** Market must be large.
3. **Will quality matter?** If "50% as good for 10% of price" wins, avoid.

## a16z's Palantirization Test

1. Where does shared product end and custom code begin?
2. Deployment timeline — engineer-months per customer?
3. Year-3 margin on mature customer?
4. What breaks at 50 customers?
5. How do you decide NOT to customize?

## Lightspeed's Five Criteria

1. Data-rich but integration-poor
2. High complexity operations
3. Mission-critical processes
4. Industry-standard workflows
5. Strong regulatory and data moats

## The Meta-Framework (Synthesized from All VCs)

**Tier 1 — Must-Haves:**
- [ ] Real workflow problem (not tech demo)
- [ ] Large industry vertical
- [ ] Gross margin trajectory improving
- [ ] Can articulate what's HARD
- [ ] Technical team
- [ ] >10 paying customers with retention evidence

**Tier 2 — Strong Signals:**
- [ ] Application-layer differentiation
- [ ] Multi-model architecture
- [ ] Quantifiable customer ROI
- [ ] Pricing aligned with value
- [ ] Path to 65%+ gross margins
- [ ] Faster execution than competitors

**Tier 3 — Nice-to-Haves:**
- [ ] Process power / workflow integration
- [ ] Proprietary data moat (or path)
- [ ] Network effects (even early)
- [ ] Industry-insider founder
- [ ] Services being productized

- **Average review time:** 2 min 24 sec (seed: 1 min 56 sec)
- **Only 12%** read in full
- **Average per slide:** 19.2 seconds

### Where Investors Spend Time (ranked)
1. **Financials & Business Model** — unit economics, does the math scale
2. **Team** — surged in importance, now #2
3. **Traction** — revenue, growth, signed pilots
4. **Why Now** — timing of opportunity

### Funded vs Non-Funded
- **100% of funded decks** had financials; only 58% of unfunded did

## The a16z Two-Deck Strategy

1. **Narrative Deck** (cold send): 10-12 slides, visual, skimmed in 2 min
2. **Data Deck** (diligence): 30+ pages, models, deep-dives

Never combine them.

## 5-Second Rule

If a slide's core message isn't verbally statable in 5 seconds, redesign it.
- "Market Size" → "The $4B API Security Market Growing 40% Annually"
- Core metric = largest element. Everything else = context.

| # | Slide | Time | Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Company Purpose | 5s | One declarative sentence |
| 2 | Problem | 10s | Broken workflow, who feels it, economic cost |
| 3 | Solution | 15s | One screenshot + one sentence |
| 4 | Why Now | 10s | Technology/market inflection |
| 5 | Market | 10s | TAM/SAM/SOM, one credible number |
| 6 | Traction | 20s | Revenue, logos, growth chart |
| 7 | Business Model | 15s | Pricing, unit economics, margin path |
| 8 | Competition | 15s | Quadrant/table, why you win |
| 9 | Team | 20s | Domain expertise, why YOU |
| 10 | Financials + Ask | 20s | Forecast, burn, runway, use of proceeds |

1. **Founders** (most important) — "I'd fund impressive founders with no idea"

## Standard Phases

### Year 1-2: Land
- Gross margin: 35-55%
- Inference: 25-35% of revenue
- Growth: 3-10x
- Burn multiple: >3x acceptable
- Focus: PMF, not profitability

### Year 3-4: Expand
- Gross margin: 55-65%
- Inference: 18-25%
- NRR: >120%
- Burn multiple: 1-2x
- Focus: Expansion revenue

### Year 5+: Optimize
- Gross margin: 65-75%
- Rule of 60+
- Path to FCF positive
- Focus: Operating leverage

## Token Cost Modeling
- Model as % of revenue that declines over time
- 30-50% cost decline per year for equivalent capability
- Usage per customer increases as features expand
- Net: inference stays ~20-25% even as per-token costs drop
- Route majority to smaller/fine-tuned models, escalate complex to frontier

## What AI COGS Actually Includes
- LLM inference, training, hosting, 3rd-party API costs
- Customer model training, user prompting
- Licensing fees for training data
- Customer support and CSM
- Infrastructure/hosting
- DevOps

## CRITICAL: Contribution Margin LTV (Not Standard SaaS LTV)

Standard SaaS LTV formula **overstates AI LTV by 20-40%** because it assumes 70-80% gross margins.

**Use Contribution Margin LTV instead:**
```
Contribution Margin LTV = (ARPU - Variable Costs per Customer) × Average Customer Lifetime
```

**Worked example (from LTVCACBook):**
- $200/month revenue, $83/month variable costs (LLM API $60 + Cloud $15 + Storage $8)
- Contribution margin: $117/month
- Standard LTV at 75% margin: $2,700 — MISLEADING
- Contribution Margin LTV: $2,106 — REAL
- At $800 CAC: Standard shows 3.4:1 (comfortable). Real shows 2.6:1 (below threshold).

**The heavy-user problem:** In SaaS, power users are best customers. In AI, power users can be most expensive. A user at 50K LLM calls/month on a $199 plan = $49 contribution margin vs. light user at $193.

## Bessemer's Three AI Business Models (Feb 2026 Pricing Playbook)

| Model | Pricing | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Copilots** | Per seat + consumption (like SaaS) | GitHub Copilot, Abridge |
| **Agents** | Outcome/workflow-based | Intercom Fin ($0.99/resolution) |
| **AI-enabled Services** | Per output/outcome | EvenUp (per demand package) |

**Practical hybrid pricing formula:**
Platform fee (2× delivery costs) + outcome credits
Example: $12K annual platform + 100 ticket resolutions included + $5K per additional 100

**Seven Guiding Principles (Bessemer):**
1. Price for value delivered, not access granted
2. Hybrid/tiered models create predictability with upside
3. Account for inference costs explicitly
4. AI reimagines traditional budgets (labor line, not IT line)
5. New success metrics (resolution rate, acceptance rate, time-to-prototype)
6. Pricing shapes entire GTM motion
7. AI = adding co-workers, not just tools

Source: https://www.bvp.com/atlas/the-ai-pricing-and-monetization-playbook

3. **Forward-Deployed Dev Shop → Enterprise Platform:** Custom software using decreasing code costs. Compete against consulting firms. Example: Distyl ($20M Series A).

# 10. RED FLAGS THAT KILL AI BUSINESS PLANS {#10-red-flags}

**INSTANT PASS for most investors:**

1. **No moat beyond "we were first"** — speed is a lead, not a moat
2. **Gross margin flat or declining** — costs not improving with scale
3. **Single model provider dependency** — API wrapper, no fallback
4. **Can't answer "what happens when GPT-6 ships"** — improvements should help, not threaten
5. **Every deployment unique** — services business, not software
6. **Horizontal, competing with labs** — "in the strike zone"
7. **TAM small even if dominant** — "great AI for VC firms"
8. **No technical co-founder**
9. **Pricing misaligned with value** — per-seat for an agent product
10. **No retention data**
11. **"We'll figure out pricing later"** — 37% already changing
12. **Claiming 80%+ margins on AI product** — lying about COGS or not doing AI
13. **"What's hard" = "our ML pipeline"** — that's a commodity
14. **Founder hasn't talked to 50+ target customers**

- CHF 100K minimum share capital (CHF 50K paid in)
- GmbH alternative: CHF 20K
- #1 Global Innovation Index
- No specific startup plan format required
- Board of directors required (at least one member Swiss resident)

## Swiss VC Market (2025 Data — Swiss Venture Capital Report 2026)

- **CHF 2.95 billion** invested in Swiss startups (+23.9% YoY)
- **ICT (software/internet):** CHF 773.6M — +150% vs terrible 2024, back to 2019 levels
- **Early stage:** CHF 1.116B (+73% YoY) — risk appetite is back
- **Seed:** CHF 298M (+23.8%)
- 32 rounds over CHF 20M — new record
- **Zurich:** CHF 1,192.5M (+89.5%)
- 74% of investors expect 25%+ increase in 2026
- Swiss = ~0.7% of global VC but punches above weight per capita

Source: https://www.startupticker.ch/assets/files/attachments/VCReport_2026_web.pdf

## Innosuisse Start-up Innovation Projects (SIP)

- Non-dilutive grants, up to 70% of project costs
- Swiss-based, <5 years, <50 FTE, scalable product
- Startup contributes ≥30%
- Combinable with VC funding
- ~16 weeks from application to decision
- **ICT pitch dates 2026:** Mar 11/25, May 20/Jun 3, Sep 9/23, Nov 25/Dec 9

**Evaluation criteria:**
1. Level of innovation (vs scientific state-of-art AND market solutions)
2. Added value in Switzerland
3. Methodological quality of project planning
4. Staff expertise
5. Sustainability + cost-benefit ratio
6. Financial capacity to fund own contribution
7. IP analysis

Source: https://www.innosuisse.admin.ch/en/start-up-innovation-projects

## European AI Context
- GDPR = constraint AND moat (compliance barrier for US competitors)
- EU Data Act right to switch — impacts lock-in
- European budgets 30-50% smaller than US
- Longer sales cycles, stickier relationships
- European investors want more detail on unit economics and path to profitability
- The "Why Switzerland?" slide matters for international investors

# 13. CROSS-FRAMEWORK SYNTHESIS {#13-synthesis}

## Where ALL Major VCs Agree

1. **Vertical AI > Horizontal** for startups
2. **Application layer** = where value accrues (49% differentiate here)
3. **Speed of execution** = #1 differentiator
4. **Gross margins will improve** (41% → 52% trajectory)
5. **Multi-model is standard** (3.1 providers avg)
6. **Hybrid pricing** emerging (subscription + consumption + outcome)
7. **Services acceptable** during platform shifts

## Where They Disagree

### Gross Margins
- **Bessemer:** 60%+ preferred
- **a16z:** Total gross profit > margin %. ServiceNow had 63% at IPO, now 79%.
- **Resolution:** Depends on WHY margins are low and trajectory.

### Services
- **a16z (Schmidt):** Correct model for complex AI. Historical proof.
- **a16z (Andrusko):** Without productization, you're "Accenture with nicer UI."
- **Resolution:** Acceptable as scaffolding. Key test: FDE effort decreasing over time?

### TAM
- **Redpoint:** Large industries preferred
- **Bessemer:** "Niche and underserved" for initial advantage
- **Resolution:** Start niche, in a large industry. Narrow wedge, wide expansion path.
