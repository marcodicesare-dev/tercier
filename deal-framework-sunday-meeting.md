## 1. Salary Ladder

The salary is an operational decision about what the company can afford to pay its CEO, not a reflection of Marco's equity value. These are separate conversations.

| Stage | Trigger | Annual Gross (CHF) | Monthly Gross | Est. Net/Mo | Monthly Burn Impact | Rationale |
|-------|---------|-------------------|---------------|-------------|--------------------|-----------|
| **Pre-revenue** | Day 0 | 60,000 | 5,000 | ~4,200 | ~9,850 (+1,000 vs current plan) | Minimum survivable in Zurich. Rent ~1,800, health insurance ~475, food ~500, transport ~200, basics ~400 = ~3,375 floor. Leaves ~825/mo buffer. Tight but functional. |
| **Early traction** | 10 hotels, MRR ≥ CHF 15K | 120,000 | 10,000 | ~7,800 | ~14,350 | Company is profitable (revenue CHF 18.2K vs burn ~14.4K). Restores Marco's current income level. Funded entirely by revenue, not seed capital. |
| **Growth** | 50 hotels, MRR ≥ CHF 75K | 180,000 | 15,000 | ~11,000 | ~19,350 | Company generating CHF 55K+/mo surplus before this raise. Market-competitive for a SaaS CEO in Zurich running ~CHF 900K ARR. |
| **Scale** | 100+ hotels, ARR ≥ CHF 2M | 200,000 + bonus | 16,667 + bonus | ~12,200 + bonus | ~21,000 | Fully market-rate. Bonus of CHF 20-50K/yr tied to specific annual targets (ARR growth %, net retention, new geo launch). |

### Salary — key negotiation points

**Why CHF 60K and not CHF 48K:** CHF 48K gross = ~CHF 3,579 net in Zurich. After rent and health insurance alone, that leaves under CHF 1,000/mo for everything else. A CEO who is stressed about groceries is a risk to the company. CHF 60K is still a CHF 60K/yr sacrifice from current income (CHF 120K) and CHF 140-190K below market (CHF 200-250K). That sacrifice over 3 years = CHF 180-570K in forgone salary. It's real. But it's not the equity argument — it's an operational argument about keeping the CEO functional.

**Why not CHF 100K from day 1:** Because it nearly doubles the burn rate and changes the seed math. At CHF 60K, monthly burn is ~CHF 9,850, breakeven at ~6 hotels, peak deficit ~CHF 70K. At CHF 100K, burn is ~CHF 13,200, breakeven at 8-9 hotels, peak deficit ~CHF 110K. The lean start is a feature, not a bug — it means less capital required, less dilution, faster path to profitability.

**Automatic vs. board-approved:** Push for automatic triggers. "When MRR hits CHF 15K for 2 consecutive months, salary adjusts to CHF 120K effective the following month." No board vote needed. This prevents a situation where the company is profitable but the founders block a salary increase.

**BVG note:** At CHF 60K, Marco is above the BVG threshold (CHF 22,680) so pension contributions apply. At CHF 48K in the original plan, he was also above it, but the employer social contributions were calculated at ~8% (CHF 320/mo on CHF 4K). At CHF 60K, employer contributions rise to ~CHF 480/mo. Factor this into burn calculations.

### Performance acceleration triggers (25% → 35%)

The +10% should vest in tranches tied to company milestones, not time:

| Milestone | Extra Equity Earned | Cumulative | Logic |
|-----------|-------------------|------------|-------|

Alternative (simpler): Full +10% acceleration to 35% on any liquidity event (exit/IPO) at ≥ CHF 50M valuation. Below that threshold, performance equity vests on the milestone schedule above.

### Equity — key negotiation points

**Why 25% is the floor, not a gift:** Marco is the ONLY full-time person. He built all the IP. He will build the product, sell the first hotels, onboard the clients, and hire the team. In a standard VC-backed startup, a CEO brought in at founding gets 5-15% — but those companies have multiple co-founders already building. Here, Marco IS the entire operating team. 25% reflects that he's joining a structure where Amedeo and Corsaro hold majority as initiators and capital providers, but contributing 100% of the execution.

**Why not 50-70%:** Because the negotiation reality is that Amedeo and Corsaro likely see themselves as the ones who "created the opportunity" (the call on March 10, the JAKALA network, the hotel pipeline). Marco's business plan positioned himself as founder at 50-70%, but that framing assumed he was the one bringing them in. If the dynamic is reversed — they're bringing him in — then 25% with performance kickers to 35% is a strong position.

**The 20% red line:** Below 20%, Marco's economic interest is too small to justify the risk. At CHF 60K salary with a CHF 200-250K market rate, he's sacrificing CHF 140-190K/yr. Over 4 years, that's CHF 560-760K in forgone compensation. If his equity at a CHF 50M exit is only 15% = CHF 7.5M, the risk/reward still works. But at 10% = CHF 5M, he could earn CHF 800K-1M in salary over the same period at zero risk. The breakeven where equity risk makes sense is around 15-20% depending on exit probability.

## 4. Marco's Protections

| Protection | What It Does | Swiss Legal Basis | Priority |
|------------|-------------|------------------|----------|
| **Anti-dilution (pre-emptive rights)** | If new shares are issued, Marco can buy proportionally to maintain his %. | Art. 652b CO — statutory right (Bezugsrecht). Can only be waived by shareholder vote with cause. | CRITICAL. This prevents Amedeo/Corsaro from issuing new shares to a third party and diluting Marco from 25% to 5%. |
| **Tag-along rights** | If Amedeo or Corsaro sell their shares, Marco can sell his on the same terms. | Contractual (shareholder agreement). Not statutory. | CRITICAL. Without this, they could sell their majority stake to a stranger who then controls the company while Marco is stuck. |
| **Board seat** | Marco has a permanent board seat as long as he holds ≥15% equity. | Contractual. Swiss AG requires at least 1 board member; no minimum for multi-member boards. | HIGH. A board seat means Marco votes on major decisions (capital raises, exits, hiring of C-level). Without it, he's an employee with equity but no governance power. |
| **Single-trigger acceleration on change of control** | ALL unvested shares vest immediately upon acquisition, merger, or IPO. | Contractual. | CRITICAL. Without this, an acquirer could fire Marco 1 day after closing and he'd lose unvested equity. |
| **Good leaver protection** | If Marco leaves for health, family, or is terminated without cause: keeps all vested shares, unvested shares vest pro-rata or at fair market value. | Contractual. Swiss courts enforce reasonable leaver clauses. | HIGH. The "bad leaver" definition must be narrow: only fraud, gross negligence, or criminal conviction. Voluntary departure after 2+ years should be "good leaver." |
| **IP pre-assignment clarity** | Work done BEFORE the company is incorporated (the 9-day sprint, data pipeline, business plan, financial model) is Marco's personal IP, licensed to the company. Work done AFTER incorporation belongs to the company. | Art. 332 CO covers employee inventions. Pre-employment IP needs explicit contractual treatment. | HIGH. If things go wrong, Marco needs to retain the IP he built before there was a company. |
| **Drag-along minimum price** | If Amedeo/Corsaro trigger a drag-along (force Marco to sell), the per-share price must be ≥ fair market value as determined by an independent valuation. | Contractual. | MEDIUM. Prevents a scenario where they sell the company cheap to a friend/related party. |
| **Information rights** | Monthly financial reporting, access to all company accounts, quarterly board meetings minimum. | Standard for any shareholder with >10%. | HIGH. Marco is CEO so he'd have this naturally, but it should be contractual in case he's ever removed from the CEO role. |
| **Removal protection** | Marco can only be removed as CEO by unanimous board vote (not simple majority). If removed without cause, triggers good leaver + 12 months salary severance. | Contractual. | HIGH. With 2 investors holding 65% combined, they could outvote Marco on the board. This clause ensures they can't just fire him. |

## 11. Revenue Allocation Strategy — When to Reinvest, Bonus, or Dividend

### Tax efficiency ranking (Zug AG, 11.85% corporate tax)

| Method | Tax treatment | Effective total tax | When to use |
|--------|--------------|--------------------|----|
| **Reinvest in company** | Taxed once at 11.85% corporate. Grows company value. Exit = 0% capital gains for Swiss shareholders. | **11.85%** (until exit) | Default mode. Always reinvest when there's productive use for the money. |
| **Salary / bonus to CEO** | Deductible for company (saves 11.85% corp tax). Taxed as personal income (~15-25% in Zug depending on amount). | **~15-25%** (personal income tax only, corp tax offset) | When Marco needs personal cash. Bonuses are tax-efficient because they reduce corporate taxable profit. |
| **Dividends to shareholders** | NOT deductible. Profit taxed at 11.85% corporate, THEN 35% WHT (refundable to ~0% for Swiss, reduced to 15% treaty for Italians), THEN personal income tax on dividends. | **~35-45%** combined effective rate for Italian shareholders | Only when company has excess cash with no productive reinvestment opportunity. Avoid until mature. |

### Why delay dividends — the math for Amedeo and Corsaro

**Scenario A: Take EUR 100K as dividend from Zug AG**
- Company earns EUR 100K profit → pays 11.85% corporate tax = EUR 11,850 → EUR 88,150 available
- Distribute as dividend → 15% treaty WHT = EUR 13,223
- Net to Italian shareholder: **EUR 74,928** (before Italian personal income tax on dividends)
- After Italian taxation (~26% on dividends): **~EUR 55,447 in pocket**

**Scenario B: Leave EUR 100K in company, take value at exit**
- Company earns EUR 100K → pays 11.85% corporate tax = EUR 88,150 retained
- EUR 88,150 reinvested grows company value. At 5-10x revenue multiple, EUR 88,150 of retained earnings could support EUR 440K-880K of valuation increase
- At exit, Italian shareholders' capital gain taxed at 26% on qualified participations
- Even without the multiplier effect, the retained EUR 88,150 is worth more at exit than the EUR 55,447 dividend in pocket

**The pitch to Amedeo and Corsaro:** "Every euro we leave in the company and reinvest is worth 2-5x more at exit than if we take it out as a dividend today. We should aim to be dividend-free for the first 3 years and take the real payday at exit."
