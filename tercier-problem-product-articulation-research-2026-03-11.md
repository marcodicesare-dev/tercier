# Tercier Problem and Product Articulation Research

Date: March 11, 2026

Purpose:
- find the sharpest problem articulation and product concept before writing the business plan
- ground the language in buyer pain, actual budgets, current market shifts, and what a premium hotel would buy fast
- avoid consultant language, generic AI language, and anything that sounds like a report business

Method:
- re-read the call transcript and internal research notes
- apply `jobs-to-be-done`, `positioning-workshop`, and `mom-test`
- use current external sources from Cloudbeds, h2c, BCG, Lighthouse, Hospitality Net, TrustYou, a16z, Bessemer, ICONIQ, YC, and PhocusWire
- pressure-test candidate positioning against four lenses:
  - hotel GM / commercial director
  - Amedeo
  - investors
  - YC

## Bottom line

The strongest articulation is not:

- premium hotels need better marketing
- hotels need better content
- hotels need AI-generated content at lower cost
- hotels need a report that tells them what to do

The strongest articulation is:

Premium and luxury hotels have direct-revenue pressure, fragmented commercial data, weak property-level execution capacity, and a new AI-discovery channel they do not control. Tercier gives each property a living commercial engine that turns local market signals, guest signals, and competitor signals into property-specific actions and publishable content fast enough to affect bookings.

That gets closer, but it is still too broad. The highest-scoring version after pressure-testing is:

**Tercier helps premium hotels win more direct bookings and higher-value guests by turning fragmented market, review, and competitor data into property-specific campaigns, pages, and content that the local team can actually ship.**

That sentence works because:

- it names the buyer outcome first: more direct bookings and higher-value guests
- it describes the input reality: fragmented data
- it makes the output tangible: campaigns, pages, content
- it implies speed and execution, not another dashboard

## What the research says is actually happening

### 1. Discovery is moving into AI, and hotels are exposed

Cloudbeds found that in AI hotel recommendations, `55.3%` of cited sources were OTAs and `72.4%` of recommended hotels were branded or large-group properties. It also found that recommended properties had broad digital footprints across YouTube, blogs, and Reddit. That means AI-mediated discovery is already favoring operators with stronger distribution, richer content, and broader web presence.  
Source: https://www.cloudbeds.com/articles/ai-hotel-recommendations-study/

BCG and NYU reported on March 2, 2026 that `37%` of travelers already use AI large language models embedded in travel sites to plan and book trips. They also point out that OTA commissions remain `15%-30%`.  
Source: https://www.bcg.com/press/2march2026-hotels-ai-reshapes-discovery-distribution-operations

Lighthouse and PhocusWire show the same directional change from another angle: the market is shifting from search-and-scroll to ask-and-book, and hotels are scrambling to make their content machine-readable, brand-safe, and usable by LLMs.  
Sources:
- https://www.mylighthouse.com/resources/blog/ai-is-changing-hotel-distribution
- https://www.phocuswire.com/hotels-AI-seo-geo-strategy

Implication:

- direct demand is under fresh pressure
- property content quality is no longer just a brand issue
- it is now a distribution issue

### 2. Hotels already feel the workflow pain, but current tools are fragmented

h2c's 2025 AI & Automation Study says `78%` of hotel chains already use AI and `89%` plan to expand AI use in the next 12 to 24 months. The demand is real. But true reliance remains low at `4.7/10`, and only `6%` have a comprehensive company-wide AI strategy. The biggest barriers are lack of AI expertise (`62%`), unclear strategy (`51%`), and integration challenges (`45%`). The top buying drivers are seamless integration (`70%`), reduction of repetitive manual tasks (`69%`), and proven revenue impact (`69%`).  
Source: https://h2c.de/work/research/2025-ai-automation-study

The h2c Direct Booking Acceleration Study says hotel chains still face OTA competition as the top barrier to direct sales, report more need for advanced IBE capabilities, still struggle with CRM data cleansing and integrations, and leave automation opportunities untapped. `43%` have no team reviewing automatable work.  
Source: https://h2c.de/wp-content/uploads/h2cs-direct-booking-acceleration-study_research-highlights.pdf

BCG adds that many hotel companies still operate with a patchwork of systems that do not integrate well, and nearly half of hoteliers report difficulty accessing critical information.  
Source: https://www.bcg.com/press/2march2026-hotels-ai-reshapes-discovery-distribution-operations

Implication:

- the pain is not "lack of AI"
- the pain is lack of an operational commercial system that can ingest signals, decide what matters, and ship something useful

### 3. Buyers spend money already, but not in a way that solves this

Hospitality Net's 2025 panel on hotel marketing budgets shows many hotels still underinvest in marketing relative to OTA pressure. Experts cite `4%-6%` of total revenue as a more serious baseline, luxury hotels up to `8%`, and direct booking cost-of-acquisition benchmarks around `10%-12%`.  
Source: https://www.hospitalitynet.org/panel/digital-marketing-in-hospitality/how-much-should-hoteliers-be-spending-on-marketing

TrustYou's official pricing shows hospitality buyers are already comfortable with point solutions:

- AI agents from about `€190/property/month`
- AI Agent product page from about `$115/property/month`
- CDP from `€350/property/month`

Sources:
- https://www.trustyou.com/pricing/ai-agents/
- https://www.trustyou.com/pricing/cdp/

These numbers matter because they prove software spend exists, but they also show the trap. Anything that sounds like review analytics, guest messaging, or another thin agent gets anchored to a few hundred euros per property per month.

Implication:

- Tercier cannot be sold as "review intelligence"
- Tercier cannot be sold as "AI content generation"
- Tercier must sit closer to commercial output and direct revenue

### 4. Enterprise AI winners are being judged on workflow ownership and proof

a16z argues that owning the workflow beats owning the model. In the AI era, the winners package orchestration and guardrails, memory and context, integrations and tools to act, and evaluation loops to prove the work got done.  
Source: https://a16z.com/anatomy-of-an-enterprise-platform-company/

Bessemer says the winning AI application pattern is to start with an AI wedge: solve a narrow, high-friction problem, deliver 10x value fast, then expand. It also says systems of action are replacing systems of record.  
Source: https://www.bvp.com/atlas/the-state-of-ai-2025

ICONIQ's 2026 State of AI says application-layer differentiation now dominates, with `49%` of companies differentiating mainly through workflows, UX, integrations, and data application. It also shows monetization shifting toward hybrid and outcome-linked pricing.  
Source: https://www.iconiq.com/growth/reports/2026-state-of-ai-bi-annual-snapshot

YC's current AI advice is blunt: demand is huge, but what wins big contracts is not vague AI positioning. It is software that can actually do the work of a person, with evals and product quality that survive enterprise scrutiny.  
Source: https://www.ycombinator.com/library/M5-ai-revolution-why-this-is-the-best-time-to-start-a-startup

Implication:

- Tercier needs a narrow initial wedge
- the product has to do work, not just show insight
- the product must prove outputs and outcomes

## Customer job to be done

### Core job

When a premium hotel needs to win the right guests at the right rate for its specific location, season, and source markets, but the local team is too thin and HQ support is too generic, it needs a way to turn live market signals and guest signals into property-specific pages, campaigns, and content fast enough to affect bookings.

### Functional job

- identify which guest segments matter now
- see how the hotel loses or wins against the comp set for those segments
- decide what messages, pages, offers, and campaigns need to change
- ship those changes without waiting on an agency or a slow internal chain

### Emotional job

- stop feeling blind and reactive
- stop guessing what matters this month
- stop signing off generic content that does not sound right for the property

### Social job

- let the GM and commercial lead feel in control of the property's market position
- let local teams look sharper than their headcount suggests
- let group leadership see a system, not chaos

## Forces of progress

### Push

- OTA pressure remains high
- AI discovery is changing how hotels are found
- local commercial teams are thin
- fragmented data and unclear priorities waste time
- agencies and HQ support are slow and generic at property level

### Pull

- one system that reads the market, guests, and competitors together
- one place to decide what matters now
- one platform that drafts pages, campaigns, and content ready for review and publishing
- one commercial rhythm instead of scattered reports and tools

### Anxiety

- fear of AI slop or brand damage
- fear of another dashboard nobody uses
- fear of messy integrations
- fear that value will be hard to prove

### Habit

- existing agencies
- existing review tools and reputation tools
- manual workflows
- content written by whoever is around

## What the buyer would actually pay for

The buyer does not care about:

- synthetic personas as an abstract concept
- competitor analysis as a PDF
- content generation in isolation
- AI orchestration as a technical story

The buyer does care about:

- stronger direct demand
- faster property-level execution
- fewer missed demand moments
- less generic content
- fewer hours wasted stitching together signals and briefs

The no-brainer buy is closest to this:

**A property-specific commercial engine that tells the team what to push, why it matters now, and gives them publishable content and campaign inputs without needing extra local headcount.**

That is still platform language. The recurring output value needs to sound more like a result.

## Best current one-sentence articulation

**Tercier helps premium hotels win more direct bookings and higher-value guests by deciding what each property should say, promote, and publish each month from live market, review, and competitor signals.**

Why this is currently the best line:

- "premium hotels" is clear enough for a wedge
- "win more direct bookings and higher-value guests" maps to revenue
- "deciding what each property should say, promote, and publish" describes the job
- "each month" implies recurring value
- "live market, review, and competitor signals" explains where the judgment comes from

Alternative if the audience is more investor-heavy:

**Tercier is a vertical AI platform for premium hotels that turns fragmented market, guest, and competitor data into property-specific commercial decisions and executable content.**

Alternative if the audience is more operator-heavy:

**Tercier tells each premium hotel what to push, what to fix, and what to publish next, then drafts the assets so the team can ship.**

## Best no-brainer output framing

The user example is the right standard. The product should not be described as "AI-powered market intelligence." It should describe the recurring result.

Best current framing:

**Each month, Tercier gives a premium hotel a ranked commercial playbook for the property, plus the pages, campaign inputs, and on-brand content drafts needed to act on it.**

That is solid, but still a little deck-like.

Sharper version:

**Tercier tells a premium hotel which guests to go after, which demand moments to exploit, which messages are weak, and hands the team ready-to-ship content and campaign inputs to act before the month is lost.**

Why this is strong:

- it is time-bound
- it sounds operational
- it implies speed and leverage
- it ties action to missed revenue risk

If a more numeric version is needed later:

- "gives each property a monthly set of prioritized demand plays and ready-to-ship content assets"
- "turns one month's scattered commercial work into one prioritized execution queue"

The exact numeric promise should not be invented yet. No source supports "X more bookings" or "Y more leads" today. That belongs in pilot validation, not in positioning.

## The actual pain point

The pain point is not "hotels need proper marketing."

The pain point is:

**Premium hotels are judged on property-level commercial performance, but most do not have property-level commercial machinery. They have fragmented data, thin teams, slow agencies or generic HQ support, and a new AI-discovery environment that punishes weak, stale, generic content.**

That pain has four parts:

1. Strategy gap

- the property does not know which guest segments and demand moments matter most right now

2. Decision gap

- nobody turns scattered signals into clear priorities fast enough

3. Execution gap

- even when the team knows roughly what to do, getting the right content and campaign assets shipped is slow

4. Distribution gap

- weak or generic content now hurts discoverability and direct booking potential in AI-mediated travel planning

## The product concept that currently scores highest

**Tercier is a property-level commercial execution platform for premium hotels. It reads live market, guest, and competitor signals, decides what matters now for each property, and drafts the pages, campaigns, and content the team needs to ship.**

Why this scores highest:

- "property-level" says why the product exists
- "commercial execution platform" avoids sounding like a report or a thin agent
- "reads live market, guest, and competitor signals" explains the engine
- "decides what matters now" describes the judgment
- "drafts the pages, campaigns, and content" shows concrete work output

This is close to 10/10.

Remaining gap:

- the product still needs one named first workflow that is easiest to buy

## The likely first wedge

After reviewing the transcript, hotel research, direct booking studies, and AI-discovery shifts, the most plausible first wedge is:

**property-specific direct-demand execution**

Meaning:

- identify the guest segments and moments to target now
- spot weak direct-channel messaging and comp-set gaps
- draft the pages, event pushes, offers, newsletters, and campaign inputs needed to react

This wedge is stronger than starting with "persona generation" or "competitive analysis" because:

- it touches revenue faster
- it is easier to demo
- it maps better to existing budgets
- it still uses the deeper platform machinery under the hood

## What not to say

- AI-powered platform for hotel marketing
- better hotel marketing with AI
- personalized content at lower cost
- marketing intelligence dashboard
- virtual marketing team for hotels
- hotel growth copilot

All of these are vague, crowded, or anchored to weak buyer categories.

Primary:

**Tercier helps premium hotels win more direct bookings and higher-value guests by deciding what each property should say, promote, and publish each month from live market, review, and competitor signals.**

Plain version:

**The platform tells each premium hotel what to push next, why it matters, and gives the team ready-to-ship content to act on it.**

## What has to be true for this to hold

This articulation is strong enough to proceed, but three things still need live validation:

1. Budget owner

- GM vs. commercial director vs. group commercial lead

2. First buying trigger

- direct booking pressure
- local-event demand capture
- weak content velocity
- AI-discovery readiness

3. Most buyable first deliverable inside the platform

- direct-channel page refreshes
- event and seasonal content engine
- comp-set and persona driven campaign planning

## Recommended next move

Before finalizing the business plan, use this articulation in `2-3` buyer conversations and test only these questions:

1. Which part of this hurts enough to buy now?
2. Which current budget would fund it?
3. Which recurring output would make the product feel essential after month one?

If buyers keep leaning toward one recurring motion, that becomes the first packaged wedge. The broader platform story stays true, but the plan and pricing should anchor on the wedge.
